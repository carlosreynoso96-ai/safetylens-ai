### Post 1 — Educational: Top 5 OSHA Violations

The top 5 most-cited OSHA violations in construction haven't changed in years.

And yet, they still account for the majority of injuries and fatalities on jobsites.

Here they are (again):

1. Fall Protection — 29 CFR 1926.501
2. Hazard Communication — 29 CFR 1910.1200
3. Scaffolding — 29 CFR 1926.451
4. Ladders — 29 CFR 1926.1053
5. Fall Protection Training — 29 CFR 1926.503

The problem isn't that safety managers don't know these exist. They do.

The problem is that jobsite conditions change hour by hour. A crew that was compliant at the morning toolbox talk can be out of compliance by lunch. Guardrails get moved. Harnesses get unclipped. PPE disappears when the super walks away.

Consistent observation is the only real defense. Whether that's daily walks, photo documentation, or using AI tools to catch what human eyes miss — the solution is always the same: more frequent, more thorough inspections.

Don't wait for OSHA to find what you missed.

Let's talk about what an OSHA citation actually costs.

The fine itself? That's just the beginning.

### Post 3 — Educational: Why Photo Documentation Matters

If it's not documented, it didn't happen.

Every safety manager knows this. But here's the reality on most jobsites:

Photos get taken on personal phones and never uploaded. Observation notes live in a spiral notebook in someone's truck. Reports get written two days after the walk — from memory.

That's not documentation. That's a liability.

Here's what good safety documentation actually looks like:

The crews that document consistently don't just pass inspections. They prevent incidents. Because the act of looking, recording, and following up changes behavior on the jobsite.

### Post 7 — Industry Insight: Construction Fatalities

Construction fatalities are still rising. Let's talk about what the data actually says.

OSHA's Fatal Four haven't changed:

- Falls: 36.5% of construction deaths
- Struck-by: 9.8%
- Electrocution: 7.2%
- Caught-in/between: 4.4%

That's nearly 60% of all construction fatalities from four preventable hazard categories.

And here's the part that should make every GC uncomfortable: the majority of these incidents happen on sites that had a safety program on paper.

The gap isn't knowledge. It's execution.

Safety programs fail when they're treated as compliance checkboxes instead of active processes. When the JSA gets signed but nobody reads it. When the toolbox talk happens but nobody walks the site after.

The companies with the lowest incident rates share one thing in common — they inspect frequently, document everything, and follow up on every observation.

Technology can help close the execution gap. AI-powered inspections, automated reporting, real-time hazard detection. But the foundation is always the same: a culture that treats safety as operations, not overhead.

### Post 8 — Industry Insight: The Skilled Labor Shortage and Safety

There's a connection between the labor shortage and jobsite safety that nobody's talking about.

When experienced workers retire, they take decades of hazard recognition with them.

A 20-year ironworker can walk onto a jobsite and instantly spot a rigging issue. A journeyman electrician can hear something wrong before they see it. That intuition doesn't transfer through a training video.

Now we're backfilling with workers who have less experience and shorter tenures. Turnover is high. Training time is compressed. And the expectation is that they perform at the same level from day one.

The result? More incidents among workers with less than one year on the job.

This isn't a criticism of new workers. It's a systems problem.

The solution has to include better tools — technology that supplements experience. AI that can look at a jobsite photo and flag the hazards that a less-experienced worker might miss. Guided safety walks that teach while they inspect.

We can't replace 30 years of field experience. But we can build tools that help close the gap while the next generation develops their own instincts.

How is your company addressing knowledge transfer as experienced workers retire?
